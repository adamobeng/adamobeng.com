Tor is Peace, Software Freedom is Slavery, Wikipedia is Truth: The Political Philosophy of the Internet

Apart from further demonstrating my ability to write ridiculously long titles, the talk I gave at the 27th Chaos Communication Congress was my attempt to combine what I knew about political philosophy and FLOSS (Free/Libre/Open-Source Software).

Because I was presenting to an audience of non-philosophers, I omitted some detail of the academic debate, and included quite a few hacker references that were not strictly necessary (but everyone loves xkcd comics!). The presentation began with an introduction to where (analytical) philosophy fits within academia, and how it might be relevant to hackers.

As the slides and lecture text show, there is much to say about political philosophy and the Internet. I also left the conclusion open-ended, because it's quite difficult to establish anything philosophical conclusively from scratch in under an hour. Also, I didn't want to start a flame-war.

The two have superficial similarities, like their focus on the names of things ("Don't call us political philosophers, we're political theorists"). My aim was to apply the methods and theories of political philosophy to a problem of real practical importance to FLOSS groups: do so-called "restrictive" FLOSS licenses really impose more restrictions on freedom?

Unsurprisingly, it depends who you ask...

Speaking in public at such an event was immense fun, as was using the methods from an abstract academic discipline on a practical subject matter (and an important one at that).
